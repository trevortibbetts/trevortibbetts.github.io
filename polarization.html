Spatial Electoral Polarization

Master's Thesis Research: Measuring Partisan Clustering Across Geographic Scales

Methods & Tools: Python, PySAL, Spatial Statistics, Graph Theory, Precinct-level election data
Status: MS Thesis / Conference Presentation

Abstract

Electoral polarization in the United States is often analyzed through ideological or institutional frameworks, yet electoral outcomes pose an inherently geographic problem. While maps reveal clear patterns of partisan clustering, most empirical measures treat geographic units as independent observations and do not formally account for spatial relationships. This thesis addresses this gap by applying a spatially explicit polarization framework based on the S statistic, which measures how similar political preferences form geographically contiguous components.

Using contemporary presidential election returns at both state and precinct scales, the analysis constructs intersection graphs that combine attribute similarity (Democratic vote share) with geographic adjacency. The framework is used to evaluate how polarization varies across spatial scales and classification schemes, comparing bipolar (red–blue) representations with multipolar categorizations of partisan intensity. The approach is further applied to a case study of California's Proposition 50 redistricting plans to examine whether changes in congressional district boundaries alter the spatial structure of representation. The results show that both plans produce the same observed spatial polarization statistic under bipolar classification, while generating slightly different permutation-based p-values due to plan-specific adjacency structures.

The results show that electoral polarization is strongly structured in space at multiple geographic scales. While S values are highest under bipolar classifications, substantial spatial polarization persists under multipolar schemes that capture intermediate partisan variation. Comparisons with traditional aspatial measures demonstrate that distribution-based metrics consistently understate the extent of polarization observed in spatial configurations.

This thesis contributes to electoral geography by introducing a formal and reproducible measure of spatial electoral polarization that captures the organization of vote returns across space. The findings highlight that understanding electoral polarization requires attention not only to how votes differ, but to how those differences are arranged and connected across the electoral landscape.

Keywords: Spatial Polarization, Electoral Geography, Graph Theory, Redistricting, S Statistic
