Measuring Spatial Electoral Polarization

Master's Thesis Research: Developing a spatially explicit index to evaluate partisan configurations across geographic scales.

Tools & Methods: Python, PySAL, GeoPandas, Graph Theory, Spatial Statistics • Keywords: Spatial Polarization, Electoral Geography, Redistricting

Project Overview

While traditional electoral analysis maps vote distributions, standard metrics often treat geographic units as independent observations without formally accounting for spatial adjacency. This research addresses that gap by introducing a spatially explicit polarization framework based on the $S$ statistic, which measures how political preferences organize into contiguous geographic components.

Significance

Understanding electoral polarization requires looking beyond aspatial, distribution-based metrics that consistently understate political division. By constructing intersection graphs that merge attribute similarity with geographic connectivity, this framework formalizes how vote differences are arranged and structured across the landscape.

Key Findings

Applying this methodology to contemporary U.S. presidential returns demonstrates that electoral polarization is intensely structured in space at multiple geographic scales. While $S$ values peak under bipolar classifications, substantial spatial polarization persists under multipolar schemes. Furthermore, redistricting evaluations reveal that while alternative boundary plans may maintain similar observed polarization statistics, they can generate distinct permutation-based significance structures driven by plan-specific adjacency networks.

2016 Presidential Precinct Bi-Polarization: Spatial distribution of Democratic and Republican voting components across the contiguous U.S. ($S = 0.9723$, $p = 0.0010$).
2020 Presidential Precinct Bi-Polarization: Multi-scale spatial configuration analysis confirming structural persistence of polarization ($S = 0.9722$, $p = 0.0001$).

Potential Extensions

Future development of this framework includes incorporating the $S$ statistic alongside complementary spatial autocorrelation measures to cross-validate regional clustering tendencies. Additionally, testing the methodology on datasets tracking members of the U.S. House of Representatives presents an opportunity to measure and visualize polarization directly within legislative and political bodies.
